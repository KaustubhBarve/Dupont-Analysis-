The Salesman Who Built Modern Finance

Origin · 1912

Before the Formula

DuPont Corporation, 1909. The company makes explosives, dyes, and paints. Its divisions run as semi-independent empires. Nobody knows which ones are actually efficient. Frank Donaldson Brown - an electrical engineer, not an accountant - is handed the problem.

The 1912 Report

In 1912, Brown filed an internal efficiency report at DuPont's Wilmington headquarters. Inside it was a mathematical observation: Return on Assets could be decomposed into two components. Net Profit Margin multiplied by Asset Turnover. Cancel the Revenue terms. You get Net Income over Total Assets. ROA - from two manageable ratios. The original DuPont model had been born.

General Motors Changes Everything

In 1918, DuPont acquired 23% of General Motors. GM was struggling. Brown was sent in. He recognized that ROA alone did not capture how debt was amplifying returns to shareholders. He added the third component: the Equity Multiplier. Total Assets divided by Shareholders' Equity. Now the formula reached all the way to ROE - what shareholders actually cared about.

From One Company to the World

Brown eventually became GM's treasurer in 1921 and joined its executive board in 1924, working alongside Alfred P. Sloan. The framework spread to industry analysts. By the 1950s it was a standard corporate performance tool. Today it is taught in every MBA programme.

The 5-Step Extension

A later academic elaboration split NPM into operating and tax components, yielding a 5-component model: Tax Burden × Interest Burden × EBIT Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier. Brown's original 3-step remains the most widely used in practice.

Brown died in 1965. His formula is still in every financial model built today.

= Net Income / Equity
5-Step: ROE = Tax Burden × Interest Burden × Operating Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier.
Tax Burden = Net Income / EBT. Interest Burden = EBT / EBIT. Operating Margin = EBIT / Revenue. The extension isolates tax and financing effects inside Net Profit Margin.
Profitability

Net Profit Margin

Net Income / Revenue

What it measures: How much of each unit of revenue survives all expenses.

Interpretation: A 15% NPM means the firm keeps ₹15 of every ₹100 in revenue.

Industry note: Pharma: 15-25%. Retail grocery: 1-3%. Both can have excellent ROE.

Efficiency

Asset Turnover

Revenue / Total Assets

What it measures: How hard the firm's asset base is working to generate revenue.

Interpretation: A 2.0x AT means ₹2 of revenue for every ₹1 of assets deployed.

Industry note: Walmart: approximately 2.5x. Apple: approximately 0.8x. Context is everything.

Leverage

Equity Multiplier

Total Assets / Shareholders' Equity

What it measures: The degree to which assets are financed by debt versus equity.

Interpretation: An EM of 3.0 means 2/3 of assets are debt-financed. ROE is amplified - and so is risk. Interest payments are fixed. Losses are amplified too.

What People Get Wrong

Correct

A high ROE driven primarily by NPM improvement is generally more sustainable than one driven by leverage increases. Margin improvement usually points to better pricing power, cost discipline, or operating efficiency.

Correct

Asset Turnover varies substantially by industry. Retail, including Walmart-style models, runs high turnover with thin margins; pharma often runs low turnover with fat margins.

Correct

DuPont analysis is useful for cross-company comparison within the same industry. The comparison loses meaning when structural business models differ too much.

Correct

Decomposition reveals which lever management has actually pulled. It separates profitability, asset efficiency, and leverage instead of treating ROE as one opaque number.

Myth

High ROE always signals a strong company. A firm with 5% NPM and 3x leverage can show 30% ROE. The leverage inflates the number and raises bankruptcy risk.

Myth

ROE equals profitability. ROE is a shareholder return metric. A company with thin margins and heavy debt can show excellent ROE while operating on the edge.

Myth

DuPont analysis only applies to manufacturing companies. It is universally applicable. It is used in banking, pharma, retail, and technology.

Myth

Increasing any component always improves the firm. Increasing the Equity Multiplier raises ROE mathematically, but it also increases financial risk and interest burden.

Myth

The higher the Asset Turnover, the better managed the company. Turnover benchmarks are industry-specific. A 0.5x turnover may be normal in heavy industry and alarming in retail.
